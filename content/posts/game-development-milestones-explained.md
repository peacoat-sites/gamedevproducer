Most games don't die because the team ran out of talent. They die because nobody knew when to stop, reassess, or kill something early enough.

Milestones are the mechanism that's supposed to prevent that. And yet, in studio after studio, I've watched them turn into performative box-checking exercises where leads scramble to demo a vertical slice that's nowhere near representative of the actual game, just so the build looks passable for a publisher review. That's not a milestone. That's theater.

Here's what I tell people who are new to production: a milestone is a decision point, not a deadline. The moment you treat it as purely a calendar event, you've lost the plot.

## What a Milestone Actually Is (and Isn't)

A milestone marks a moment in development where the project should be able to answer a specific set of questions. Can we make this? Does this feel fun? Is the core loop working? Are we on pace to ship within budget? Every milestone should be designed around questions you genuinely don't have answers to yet.

That sounds obvious. It isn't practiced nearly as often as you'd think.

What milestones are not: they're not a signal that you've completed a phase, they're not a trophy, and they're not primarily for the publisher or the executive team. They're for the team. A good milestone forces you to surface what's working and what's broken, on a cadence that's tight enough to course-correct.

I made this mistake myself on a mid-sized mobile project around 2017. We had a milestone calendar inherited from a contract template, and we hit every single one of them on time. Game shipped in a state that nobody was proud of. The milestones were there; the honest evaluation wasn't.

## The Standard Milestone Gates, Explained

The game industry doesn't have one universal standard, but there's a common structure that most AAA studios and publishers recognize. Indie teams adapt it. Work-for-hire studios live and die by it. Here's how it usually runs:

**Concept / Pitch**

This is pre-production's starting gun. You're delivering a vision document, sometimes a playable proof of concept, and a high-level budget estimate. The question being answered here is simple: is this idea worth investing development resources in? At this stage, the bar isn't polish. It's convincing yourself (and anyone holding the budget) that this is worth pursuing seriously.

**Prototype**

Often the most undervalued milestone in the entire pipeline. A working prototype doesn't mean a demo you'd show press. It means you've proven that the core mechanic can exist in playable form. This is where your assumptions get tested for the first time, and you should expect many of them to be wrong. Teams that skip or rush prototyping are setting themselves up for pain during production.

**Pre-Production Complete / Greenlight**

This is the gate that separates pre-production from full production. You should be able to walk into greenlight with a target platform list, a feature set, a scope that matches your budget, a production schedule you actually believe in, and a vertical slice that shows the game's core experience at something close to target quality. A vertical slice is one small piece of the game done well, not a trailer-friendly moment you'll never replicate.

Publishers often hold back a significant portion of funding until greenlight. That's intentional. It's the clearest forcing function the industry has for making teams commit to a real plan.

**Alpha**

Alpha means all features are in, even if they're not complete. Content may be missing or placeholder. The game is feature-complete in the sense that the structure exists, but it's rough. The question alpha answers is: does everything we said we'd build actually exist in some playable form?

Here's where teams often lie to themselves. They'll call something alpha because the calendar says so, when in reality a third of the planned features still aren't implemented. Don't do this. A false alpha just compresses your beta and kills your team during crunch.

**Beta**

Beta is content-complete. All levels are in, all features are tuned, all assets are final or close to it. The game is now being tested for bugs, not for design changes. Ideally, design lock happens before beta, not during it. Feature creep that sneaks into beta is the single fastest way to extend a ship date.

**Gold / Submission / Release Candidate**

This is the end of the road for platform submissions. The build is bug-free enough for certification. For console, that means passing platform holder requirements (Sony, Microsoft, Nintendo all have their own TRC/TCR checklists). For PC, your bar is different but your responsibility to players isn't.

And then you ship.

## Why Teams Get This Wrong

The gap between what milestones are supposed to do and what they actually do in practice comes down to a few honest problems.

First, the definitions drift. Every studio, publisher, and lead has a slightly different understanding of what "alpha" means. I've been in rooms where two senior producers argued for twenty minutes about whether a game was in alpha or beta. Define your milestone criteria at the start of pre-production, in writing, and get everyone to sign off on it. Sounds bureaucratic. Saves months.

Second, milestones get treated as pass/fail events instead of as conversations. A missed milestone shouldn't be a crisis. It should be data. You need to know why it was missed, whether the plan needs to change, and what's going to be different going forward. The milestone review meeting is probably the most important recurring conversation a production team has, and it's often the one that gets handled the worst.

Third, and this one's uncomfortable: sometimes the people reviewing milestones have incentives that don't align with honest assessment. Publishers want projects to keep moving so they protect their investment. Studio executives want to reassure their board. Leads don't want to be the person who called a red flag. The result is everyone in the room knowing the milestone wasn't really met, but agreeing to move on anyway. I've seen it happen at studios with hundreds of people.

If you're using a tool like Jira, Shortcut (formerly Clubhouse), or Hack n Plan (which is built specifically for game dev) to track your milestone criteria, you at least have a fighting chance at honest reporting. The data's harder to spin than a verbal check-in.

## Pre-Production Deserves Way More Time Than You're Giving It

This could be its own article, but since milestones are the structure around it, I'll say it here.

Most independent and mid-tier teams massively underinvest in pre-production. They want to get to making the game. They're excited. The prototype felt great. The temptation to just start building is enormous, and "we'll figure it out as we go" sounds agile but it's usually just avoidance.

Pre-production should last long enough to answer every major unknown about your game. What's the core loop? Does it actually feel good? What's the art style at scale? What's the technical risk? Where are the systems that might not work?

The vertical slice is your best tool here, and it's worth doing properly. Pick one representative section of your game and build it to as close to shipping quality as you can. Not a scripted demo. An actual playable section where the design, art, and code all have to coexist and work. The problems that surface during a vertical slice are the exact problems that will eat your production if you don't catch them early.

Obsidian Entertainment is a studio I've watched navigate this well in the documentary and interview coverage around their projects. They talk openly about the tension between pitching ambitious scope and committing to something achievable. That tension is real for everyone, regardless of team size.

## Tools and Resources Worth Having in Your Corner

If you're a producer trying to actually build a milestone system that functions, a few things genuinely help.

For project management, Hack n Plan was built by game developers for game developers, and it handles the specifics of game dev workflows better than Jira does out of the box. That said, Jira with a well-configured setup and proper labeling is what most mid-to-large studios use, and knowing it is valuable for your career regardless of your personal preference.

For building your actual production knowledge, "The Game Production Handbook" by Heather Maxwell Chandler is the closest thing the industry has to a textbook. Third edition is the one worth buying. If you want something more honest about the chaos of actual development, Jason Schreier's "Blood, Sweat, and Pixels" will give you the human side of what goes wrong and why milestones matter in the first place.

Coursera and LinkedIn Learning both have game production courses worth taking if you're newer to the role. I'd specifically look for anything taught by someone who shipped a game, not just someone who studied production management academically.

The thing about milestones is that they only work if everyone agrees to be honest inside them. The structure is the easy part. A spreadsheet, a checklist, a date on a calendar. The hard part is building a team culture where calling a red flag early is safer than pretending everything's fine.

That's less about production methodology and more about what kind of environment you're creating. The best milestone system in the world fails when the humans using it are afraid to tell the truth.
