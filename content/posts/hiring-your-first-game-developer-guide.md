Most first-time game studio founders hire wrong. Not because they don't care, but because they hire the person they *like talking to* rather than the person who can actually ship the thing they're trying to build. I watched three separate indie studios collapse in 2021 and 2022 partly because of that exact mistake, two of them people I'd worked alongside. Liking someone is not a hiring criterion. Let's get into what actually is.

## Know What You're Buying Before You Shop

Before you post a single job listing, you need brutal clarity on one question: what does your game require in the next six months, specifically?

Not what would be *nice*. What is actually blocking you from shipping a vertical slice or hitting your next funding milestone. If you're building a narrative adventure in Twine and you personally can't write dialogue systems, you need a programmer who knows JavaScript or Harlowe. If you're making a physics-heavy platformer in Unity and your prototype is held together with Stack Overflow answers, you need a mid-level generalist who can refactor a mess. If you've never built a build pipeline and your game is releasing on three platforms, that's a specific hire.

Most people posting "game developer wanted" ads have skipped this step entirely. Their listing reads like a wish list for a fictional senior engineer who also does UI, audio, and "a bit of design." That person either doesn't exist or costs $140,000 a year and isn't answering your Itch.io forum post.

Write down the three things that, if undone, mean your game doesn't ship. That's your actual job description.

## Where to Actually Find Candidates

Job boards work, but you need to pick the right ones. For indie games specifically:

Itch.io has a [jobs board](https://itch.io/jobs). It's small, it's free, and the people browsing it know what indie game development actually looks like. No one on Itch.io thinks they're getting a AAA salary. That's useful when you're working with limited budget.

Discord is genuinely your best first stop. Servers like Game Dev League, r/gamedev's Discord, and engine-specific servers (Unity's official server, Godot's community server) have dedicated job posting channels. Post there before you pay for anything.

LinkedIn works for finding senior or mid-level people who want to move from a studio into something smaller, but expect to sift. The signal-to-noise ratio is rough.

Reddit's r/gamedev and r/gamedevclassifieds are hit or miss depending on your budget transparency. If you're paying below market, say so upfront and explain why (equity, passion project, revenue share). People who are in for those reasons will self-select. Hiding your budget until the fourth email wastes everyone's time and poisons the relationship before it starts.

Handshake is worth considering if you're open to recent graduates. A junior programmer fresh out of DigiPen or Full Sail who has shipped even one student project is worth talking to.

## Reading Portfolios Without Getting Fooled

Here's where most non-technical founders get burned. You look at someone's portfolio, you see a polished game, you assume they built it. Sometimes they did. Sometimes they were the solo dev. Sometimes they were one of twelve people and their contribution was a single system that another engineer actually designed.

Ask specific questions during the first conversation. Not "tell me about your best project." Instead: "Walk me through one technical problem you personally solved on that project. What broke, what did you try first, what actually worked?" Anyone who built the thing can answer that in detail. Anyone who's padding their resume will give you a vague answer or pivot to talking about the team.

For programmers, look at GitHub. Not just *whether* they have one, but the commit history. Consistent commits over months tells you more than a polished demo. One repo with 400 commits in a two-week burst followed by nothing is a warning sign.

For artists, ask to see the ugly stuff: the early blockout, the first concept pass, the thing that didn't make it into the game. People who understand craft can talk about their failures. People who can only show you the finished portfolio piece are riskier.

If you're hiring a programmer and you have any technical ability at all, give them a small paid test. Not spec work, not "build a feature for free." Pay them $150-300 for 3-5 hours to solve a scoped problem that resembles what they'd actually work on. A 2D movement controller in Unity. A Godot shader that does X. You'll learn more about working with that person in one test than in six interviews.

## The Contractor vs. Employee Question

For a first hire at an indie studio, hire a contractor unless you have a compelling reason not to. I know that's not the answer some people want, but it's honest.

Employees come with payroll taxes, benefits obligations, state-specific compliance requirements, and in some jurisdictions, strong protections that make ending a bad fit expensive and slow. If you're a two-person LLC with $80,000 in seed money or Kickstarter funds, you cannot afford an employment mistake.

Contractors give you flexibility. You can scope a contract to a milestone, a feature, a vertical slice. If it goes well, you expand. If it doesn't, you pay out the contract and move on. Tools like Deel or Gusto make contractor payments and 1099 generation straightforward for US-based studios; Deel handles international contractors well if your hire isn't local.

The main exception: if you have meaningful funding (let's say $500,000 or more), you're building a team of five or more, and you want someone deeply invested in long-term success, a full-time employee with equity makes more sense. Just don't skip the legal setup. Use a service like Clerky or work with an actual games industry attorney (Zachary Strebeck is well-known in indie game legal circles) to get your offer letters, IP assignment agreements, and contractor agreements right from the start.

Speaking of IP: every agreement, contractor or employee, needs a clear work-for-hire or IP assignment clause. This is the single most common legal mistake I see first-time studio founders make. Code, art, music written on your dime belongs to your studio, in writing, before a single file is created.

## Running the Actual Interview

Skip the brain teasers. Skip the "where do you see yourself in five years" question. You're not a Fortune 500 company and pretending to run a corporate hiring process is going to feel hollow to everyone in the room.

What actually matters for a small game team:

**Can they work with uncertainty?** Small indie projects change constantly. Scope shifts, design pivots, features get cut. Ask directly: "Tell me about a time a project you were on changed direction significantly. What was your reaction, and how did you adapt?" Candidates who describe pivots as exciting or at least manageable are different from candidates who describe them as frustrating or someone else's fault.

**How do they handle feedback?** Show them something from your current build, ideally something you know has problems. Ask what they'd do differently. You want someone who can engage critically with your work without being cruel and without being sycophantic. Both extremes are bad on a two-person team.

**Can they communicate asynchronously?** If you're working with a remote contractor, their Slack/Discord communication matters as much as their code. Ask about their preferred working style. Have they worked remotely before? What tools did they use? Do they send long detailed updates or short check-ins?

References still matter. Ask for two people they've shipped something with, not managers, collaborators. Ask those references one honest question: "Was there ever a point in the project where they frustrated you, and how did they handle it?" That question gets you real information. "Would you work with them again?" gets you yes or no.

## What to Actually Pay

This is the section most guides skip because it's awkward. Don't be awkward. Money is information.

Contractor rates for game developers in the US in 2024 range widely. A junior Unity programmer with one shipped title is going to run $35-55/hour. A mid-level engineer with 3-5 years and a solid portfolio is $65-100/hour. Senior generalists who know your stack deeply can hit $120-150/hour or higher. Those numbers shift downward for candidates outside major metro areas and outside the US.

Revenue share deals are fine as a *supplement* to real pay but not a replacement for it. "50% of profits when we launch" is not compensation. It's a lottery ticket. If you don't have the budget to pay something meaningful, be transparent about that and find a co-founder instead of pretending it's a job.

For tools to track budget and stay on top of your hiring costs, HackerOne's no-code spreadsheet approach works fine at the start. Once you're managing multiple contractors, something like Notion with a proper contractor tracking template, or Airtable, keeps things organized without $50/month software overhead. For project management once the hire is made, Linear is genuinely excellent for small game dev teams. Flat enough for one or two people, structured enough to not become chaos at ten.
