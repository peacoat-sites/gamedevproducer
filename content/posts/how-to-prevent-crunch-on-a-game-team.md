Crunch killed two of my closest friends in the industry. Not literally, but one left games entirely after shipping a AAA title that required 80-hour weeks for eight months straight, and the other had a health scare at 34 that her cardiologist directly linked to sustained stress and sleep deprivation. I've watched talented producers, engineers, and artists grind themselves into dust on games that didn't even ship well. And the part that still makes me angry? Most of that crunch was preventable.

Not all of it. I want to be honest upfront: there are moments in production where you hit an unexpected technical wall, or a platform holder changes a certification requirement two weeks before launch, and you're going to ask people to dig deep for a short stretch. That's real. But the chronic, multi-month crunch that's become normalized in this industry? That's almost always a planning failure, a scope failure, or a leadership failure. Sometimes all three at once.

Here's what most people don't realize: crunch doesn't appear in the final month. It's baked in six months earlier, in decisions that felt reasonable at the time.

## Scope Is Where Crunch Is Born

I've sat in enough project kickoffs to recognize the pattern. Someone pitches a feature set. Everyone nods. Nobody does the math. And "the math" isn't just hours, it's the dependency chain, the iteration cycles, the QA pass time, the localization overhead, the platform-specific work that always gets underestimated by a factor of two.

The single most effective thing a producer can do to prevent crunch is run a brutal scope audit before a single line of production code is written. Take every feature on the list and ask three questions: what does "done" actually mean for this feature, how long did a similar feature take on the last project (not how long you hope this one takes), and what breaks if we cut it. If you can't answer all three, that feature isn't ready to be scheduled.

I'm a big believer in planning poker for estimation, specifically because it forces the people doing the work to own the numbers. Tools like Jira, Shortcut (formerly Clubhouse), or even a well-structured spreadsheet can support this, but the tool isn't what matters. What matters is that your engineers and artists are estimating their own tasks, not having estimates handed down to them. Top-down scheduling is one of the most reliable crunch generators in existence.

Scope creep is the other half of this. A feature added in month four without anything being removed is a promise made against time you don't have. I'm not saying never add anything. Games are creative and things change. But every addition needs a corresponding conversation about what comes out or what timeline moves. If that conversation isn't happening, you're building a debt that gets paid in crunch.

## Build the Schedule Like Something Will Go Wrong (Because It Will)

A schedule with no buffer is not a schedule. It's a wish list.

I target 20-25% buffer in my milestone plans, and honestly even that gets eaten alive sometimes. Platform certification delays, a key contractor going dark, an engine update that breaks your physics system, a pandemic (yes, really): the list of things that can go sideways is long, and the ones that actually hit you are usually the ones you didn't anticipate.

The way I structure this in practice: I plan in two-week sprints, I hold a "risk register" that gets reviewed every sprint, and I keep a list of features tagged as "cut candidates" at all times. Not because I plan to cut them, but because if something blows up in week 14, I need to make a scope decision in 24 hours without having to do the analysis from scratch.

If you're using Agile or Scrum on your team (and you probably should be, with some adaptation for game dev workflows), the velocity data from your early sprints is genuinely useful here. After three or four sprints you start to see what your team's real throughput is, not the theoretical throughput you planned against. Adjust your schedule to the real number. That adjustment, as uncomfortable as it is to make, is what prevents a six-week crunch.

One resource I keep recommending: *The Art of Project Management* by Scott Berkun is old but it's got some of the clearest thinking I've found on building realistic plans. For game-specific framing, Heather Maxwell Chandler's *The Game Production Handbook* is thorough and practical.

## Overtime Isn't a Productivity Tool

This is where I'll stake out a clear position: mandatory sustained overtime does not improve output. It degrades it.

There's solid research behind this, and it lines up with everything I've observed firsthand. After about four weeks of consistent 55-plus hour weeks, productivity per hour drops enough that you're essentially getting the same output you'd get from 40 hours, except your people are burning out in the process. After eight weeks, you can actually see productivity decline in absolute terms. Tired developers introduce more bugs. Tired artists stop catching quality issues. Tired QA testers miss regressions. You're building technical debt at the exact moment you can least afford it.

The game industry has historically treated this as a cost of doing business. I think that framing is changing, slowly, but it's changing. The unionization movement, the public post-mortems from studios like Bungie and CD Projekt Red, the increased employee leverage after the 2021-2022 layoff cycles pushed some experienced developers into demanding better conditions: all of it is creating pressure for more honest production practices.

What actually works: keeping sprints to sustainable velocity, being transparent with your team about where the schedule stands (don't hide the bad news, it always comes out anyway), and making scope calls before you're in crisis. When people trust that leadership will cut features rather than demand blood, they work steadier and better.

## The Culture Problem Nobody Wants to Talk About

You can have the best Jira board on earth and still have a crunch culture. Because crunch isn't only a scheduling problem. It's also a status problem.

In a lot of studios, working late is visible and praised. Leaving at 5pm is invisible or subtly stigmatized. Leads and directors model 60-hour weeks and wonder why their teams feel pressure to do the same. This is one of those things that comes entirely from the top, and it's one of the reasons that crunch reform is almost impossible to achieve without leadership buy-in at a senior level.

If you're a producer in a mid-level role, your options here are more limited, but not zero. You can protect your immediate team. You can make a point of leaving at a normal time yourself and being visible about it. You can give direct feedback when a milestone gets met without overtime and call it a win publicly. Small things, but they accumulate.

If you're in a leadership position, the calculus is different. You need to explicitly name crunch as a risk to the project (because it is), track overtime hours in your sprint reviews the same way you'd track velocity, and make it clear that approving crunch as a default is not acceptable behavior from leads. Not a suggestion. A directive.

Tools like Clockify or Toggl can help teams track actual hours worked without making it feel punitive, if you frame it correctly. The goal is visibility, not surveillance. When you can see that a team member is consistently logging 55-hour weeks, you can have a real conversation about it before they quit or flame out.

## Late-Stage Crunch: When It's Already Happening

Sometimes you're reading this after you're already in it. Fair. Let's talk about that.

If you're in active crunch, the first thing to do is triage honestly. Not optimistically. Make a list of everything that has to be in the build versus everything that would be nice to have, and be ruthless about the second category. A feature nobody plays is not worth someone's health.

Second: communicate the situation to your team clearly and honestly. Tell them what you know, what you don't know, and what you're doing about it. People can endure hard stretches when they feel respected and informed. What makes crunch genuinely demoralizing is when leadership pretends everything is fine while the sprint board is on fire.

Third, and I say this knowing it's hard to hear: if the crunch is being driven by external pressure (a publisher mandate, a release date set before the game was properly scoped), somebody needs to have that conversation with whoever is holding the deadline. It might mean pushing the date. It might mean cutting the feature set. It might mean a difficult call with a partner. But making that call in October is survivable. Burning out your whole team by December and shipping a broken game is not.
