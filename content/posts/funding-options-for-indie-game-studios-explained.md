Most indie studios run out of money before they run out of ideas. That's not a pessimistic take -- it's just the most common way games don't get finished. And if you're reading this, you're probably trying to figure out how to make sure that doesn't happen to you.

Here's what I tell people who ask me about funding: there's no single right answer, and anyone who leads with "just get a publisher deal" or "just do Kickstarter" is giving you a shortcut that might not fit your situation at all. The funding path that works for a two-person team making a narrative puzzle game is genuinely different from the one that works for a solo dev building a tactics RPG with 200 hours of content. Context matters enormously here.

So let's go through the real options -- not the fantasy versions, the actual ones.

## Bootstrapping (And Why Most People Do It Longer Than They Should)

Almost every indie studio starts here. You use savings, keep a day job, maybe cut your living expenses down to the bone. This is the most common funding model in indie games, and it's also the one that quietly destroys the most projects.

Not because it's wrong. Because people do it longer than they should without a plan for when it ends.

If you're bootstrapping, you need to know two numbers cold: your monthly burn rate and your runway. Burn rate is what you spend each month on the game (including the portion of your own salary you're not paying yourself, which is a real cost even if it doesn't show up in your bank account). Runway is how many months you can keep going before you hit zero. Most people I've talked to don't actually know these numbers. They know roughly, which is not the same thing.

Tools that help here: a simple Airtable or Notion budget tracker works fine. I've also seen teams use the free tier of Forecast.app to model out scenarios. Nothing fancy required, but you need to actually do it.

The honest ceiling on bootstrapping is usually $50,000-$150,000 of real spending before people hit a wall. Some go higher. For a lot of games -- especially smaller ones -- that's enough. But if your game needs more than 18-24 months of sustained full-time development from two or more people, you're probably going to need outside money at some point. Plan for that before the wall appears, not after.

## Grants: The Underused Option Most Devs Don't Know About

This is where I think most indie devs leave money on the table. Grants don't require giving up equity. They don't require you to repay the money. And in many cases, they're specifically designed for small creative teams making exactly the kind of games you're probably making.

The Canada Media Fund, the UK Games Fund, and Australia's Screen Australia all run grant programs for game developers. If you're in the US, the situation is messier -- there's no federal equivalent -- but state-level arts funding exists (New York, Washington, and Illinois have been more active than most), and organizations like the Independent Games Festival don't give cash grants but winning can unlock doors to other funding.

The Epic MegaGrants program is worth knowing about. As of 2024, they've handed out over $73 million USD total, in amounts ranging from $5,000 to $500,000. You don't have to use Unreal Engine to apply, which surprises people. The application is a real time investment, but it's not equity-free money you should ignore.

What makes grants hard is the timeline. Most take 3-6 months from application to decision, and some have specific intake windows. You also need to be comfortable writing applications that frame your game in terms of cultural or artistic merit, which feels weird if you've never done it. My suggestion: read five successful grant applications before you write yours. Some organizations publish them. That alone will save you weeks of confusion.

A few grant programs I'd put on your radar depending on where you're based:

- Epic MegaGrants (global, no equity, $5K-$500K)
- UK Games Fund (UK studios, currently up to £25,000 for early stage)
- Canada Media Fund Experimental Stream (Canadian companies, can go into six figures)
- Nordic Game Program (Nordic and Baltic studios)
- IndieCade and similar festival programs (smaller amounts, but legitimizing)

## Publisher Deals: What You're Actually Trading

Publisher deals are not free money. That sounds obvious, but I've seen devs sign publisher agreements without fully understanding what they gave away.

The structure of a typical indie publisher deal looks like this: the publisher advances you money to finish development (anywhere from $50,000 to a few million, depending on the publisher and the game's scope), and in exchange they take a revenue share -- often 70/30 or 60/40 in their favor -- until the advance is recouped, after which it might flip to something more favorable to you. They may also take a marketing fee that comes out before revenue share is calculated. Some take worldwide rights. Some take sequel rights. Some take both.

Devolver Digital, Raw Fury, Humble Games, Fellow Traveller, Annapurna Interactive -- these are the names that come up most in indie circles, and their reputations vary. Annapurna has had very public contract disputes with developers. Raw Fury has a decent reputation for founder-friendly terms. Devolver is selective to the point where getting their attention is itself a project. None of these are definitively "good" or "bad" publishers -- it depends on your specific deal.

Here's the thing most people don't ask: what happens if the game underperforms? Who eats that? What's the reversion clause if they go bankrupt or drop your game during marketing? These aren't paranoid questions. They're standard contract questions, and you need a games-industry entertainment lawyer to review any deal before you sign it. Spending $1,500-$3,000 on legal review is not optional if you're signing a publishing agreement. It's insurance on your company.

If you want to go deeper on the business side of publisher relationships, Thomas Bidaux does excellent public data analysis on the industry, and the Game Developers Conference vault (the GDC Vault, which has a substantial free tier) has sessions from developers talking about their specific publisher experiences.

## Crowdfunding: Harder Than It Looks, Possible If You Prepare

Kickstarter is not a lottery. The campaigns that succeed at or above their funding goals aren't just lucky -- they typically have an existing audience before they launch, a page that communicates the game clearly in under 60 seconds of video, and a funding goal that's set based on what they actually need, not what sounds impressive.

The average successful game Kickstarter in 2023 raised around $40,000-$60,000. That's not a typo. Most people imagine Kickstarter as the path to a $2 million windfall, and while those exist (Bloodstained, Shovel Knight, Divinity: Original Sin), they are serious outliers built by teams with existing fanbases or industry credibility. If you have neither of those things yet, set your expectations accordingly.

Kickstarter takes 5% of funds raised, payment processors take another 3-5%, and you'll likely spend 20-30% of your time for 30-45 days running the campaign. It's a real job.

Fig and BackerKit Crowdfunding are alternatives worth knowing, especially if your game has a tabletop or collector's market crossover. Indiegogo has fallen off significantly for games specifically.

The best use of crowdfunding for most indie studios isn't actually to fund the whole game. It's to fund a specific milestone -- the next 6 months, a specific content chunk, the console port -- while proving market demand to potential investors or publishers at the same time. Treat it as validation plus partial funding, and the math works out better.

## Angel Investment and Revenue-Based Financing

This is less talked about in indie circles, but it exists.

Angel investors are individuals who invest their own money in early-stage companies. Some of them play games, like games, and will write checks to indie studios if they believe in the team and see a path to return. The tradeoff is equity: you're giving up a percentage of your company, which means giving up that percentage of any future sale or revenue. If your studio sells for $10 million in 7 years and you gave an angel 15% for $75,000 in funding, that was a reasonable trade. If it never sells, you've diluted yourself for nothing.

Finding angels specific to games is harder than it sounds. The games-focused VC and angel community is small. BITKRAFT Ventures, London Venture Partners, and Konvoy are the names that come up in games-specific VC, though they're usually looking at later-stage or higher-concept studios. For early angels, talking to people in your existing network who have disposable investment capital is more realistic than cold-pitching firms.

Revenue-based financing is a newer option that a few companies now offer specifically to game studios. Mainshares, Clearco (formerly Clearbanc), and a handful of boutique lenders will advance you money against projected future revenue, and you repay it as a percentage of your actual revenue until the amount is paid back plus a fee. No equity lost, but you're betting on your revenue projections being accurate. For studios with a game already generating money -- a mobile title, a PC game in early access -- this can actually be a useful bridge. For pre-revenue studios, most of these lenders won't touch you.

## Early Access and Pre-Sales

Steam Early Access is one of the most underrated funding mechanisms in indie games and one of the most frequently misused.

The ones that work have a game that's genuinely playable, a community that's been building for months before launch, and clear communication about what's done and what isn't. The ones that fail have a vertical slice, no audience, and a vague roadmap. Reviewers and players can tell the difference, and bad early access reviews are very hard to recover from.

If you have 6+ months of content playable and a Discord or mailing list with at least a few thousand engaged people, Early Access is worth considering seriously. If you don't, build the audience first. That's not a detour -- that's actually part of the job.
