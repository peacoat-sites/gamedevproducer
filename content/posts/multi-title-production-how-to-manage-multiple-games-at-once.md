You're staring at three different project boards, each one representing a game in active development. Team A needs a sprint review. Team B's lead just flagged a critical blocker. Team C is two weeks from a [publisher milestone](/publisher-vs-self-publishing-what-changes-for-producers/) and someone just quit. This is multi-title production, and if nobody warned you it would feel like this, you're not alone.

Managing multiple games simultaneously is one of the hardest skill jumps in game development. Single-title production is hard enough. Multi-title adds a whole different layer of cognitive load, people management complexity, and strategic decision-making that most producers learn by surviving it rather than studying it.

Here's what actually helps.

## Build a Portfolio View Before You Need One

The biggest mistake I see is producers managing each title in isolation, then zooming out only when something breaks. By then you're reactive, constantly putting out fires instead of seeing them coming.

You need a portfolio layer sitting above your individual project boards. Your command center. It doesn't track every task. It tracks health.

Your portfolio view should show you, at a glance:

- Current milestone and days to deadline for each title
- Red/yellow/green status for scope, budget, and team morale
- Shared dependencies or shared team members across projects
- Upcoming risk flags ([playtests, platform submissions](/platform-certification-what-producers-need-to-know/), key personnel travel)

**Notion** works well for lightweight portfolio dashboards. **Confluence** paired with **Jira** if you need tighter integration. Or honestly, a well-structured **Google Sheet** if you're keeping it lean. The tool matters less than updating it every single week without fail.

## Protect Shared Resources Like They're Irreplaceable (Because They Are)

Shared team members are the single biggest source of multi-title chaos. The audio lead who's supposed to be 50% on Game A and 50% on Game B is, in practice, often 80% on whichever project is yelling loudest that week.

The fix isn't a perfect org chart. It's explicit allocation agreements with clear escalation rules.

Write it down. "Alex is available to Game B for no more than 12 hours per week during Q3." When Game A needs Alex for more than that, it goes through you, not through a Slack at 11pm. Track allocation in real time. **Float** is built for this. **Teamwork** handles resource scheduling well too. When someone is consistently over-allocated across projects, that's not a people problem. It's a planning problem you own.

## Run Separate Rhythms, Share One Cadence

Each game gets its own sprint cycle, its own standups, its own retrospectives. Don't collapse these. A Game A problem discussed in a Game B standup wastes everyone's time.

But you also need one shared cadence at the producer level. I run a weekly portfolio sync, 30 minutes, just with leads. We look at the dashboard. We surface cross-project issues. We make resource tradeoff decisions in that room instead of in hallway conversations.

This keeps each team feeling like their project matters while giving you the visibility to manage tradeoffs fairly.

## Decision Triage: How to Prioritize When Everything Feels Urgent

Not every urgent thing is important, and not every important thing feels urgent.

Use this framework for cross-title decisions:

| Priority | Criteria | Response Time |
|---|---|---|
| P1 | Blocks a milestone, submission, or launch | Same day |
| P2 | Impacts team velocity or morale within the next sprint | Within 48 hours |
| P3 | Important but can be scheduled | Next weekly sync |
| P4 | Nice to have, no current deadline pressure | Backlog review |

When you get a Slack saying "we need to talk about the combat system," that's not automatically P1. Train your leads to label requests before they hit you. It saves enormous mental bandwidth.

## What to Read and Learn Right Now

If you want to go deeper on the systems side:

- **"The Art of Game Design" by Jesse Schell** won't teach you production directly, but it builds the design literacy that makes you a better partner across titles.
- **"Agile Game Development" by Clinton Keith** is the closest thing to a textbook for this environment. Practical and honest.
- **Project Management Professional (PMP) certification** or, more specifically for games, **the PMI-ACP** track covers portfolio management concepts that transfer well.
- **MasterClass on Game Design by Will Wright** gives you design context that helps you ask smarter scope questions.
- **GDC Vault** has multiple sessions on production at scale. Free, searchable by topic.

---

Multi-title production doesn't have to be controlled chaos. It's a learnable discipline built on systems, clear communication, and the willingness to say "that's not a P1" out loud, even when people wish it were. Start with the portfolio view. Protect your shared resources. Build the deputy relationships before you need them. The teams depending on you deserve a producer who can see the whole board, not just the loudest square on it.
