You're staring at three different project boards, each one representing a game in active development. Team A needs a sprint review. Team B's lead just flagged a critical blocker. Team C is two weeks from a publisher milestone and someone just quit. This is multi-title production, and if nobody warned you it would feel like this, you're not alone.

Managing multiple games simultaneously is one of the hardest skill jumps in game development. Single-title production is hard enough. Multi-title adds an entirely different layer of cognitive load, people management complexity, and strategic decision-making that most producers learn by surviving it rather than studying it.

Here's what actually helps.

---

## Build a Portfolio View Before You Need One

The most common mistake I see is producers who manage each title in isolation and only zoom out when something breaks. By then, you're reactive. You're constantly putting out fires instead of seeing the fire coming.

You need a portfolio layer sitting above your individual project boards. Think of it as your command center. It doesn't track every task. It tracks health, not activity.

Your portfolio view should show you, at a glance:

- Current milestone and days to deadline for each title
- Red/yellow/green status for scope, budget, and team morale
- Shared dependencies or shared team members across projects
- Upcoming risk flags (playtests, platform submissions, key personnel travel)

Tools that work well here: **Notion** for lightweight portfolio dashboards, **Confluence** paired with **Jira** for studios that need more integration, or even a well-structured **Google Sheet** if you're keeping it lean. The tool matters less than the discipline of actually updating it weekly.

---

## Protect Shared Resources Like They're Irreplaceable (Because They Are)

Shared team members are the single biggest source of multi-title chaos. The audio lead who's 50% on Game A and 50% on Game B is, in practice, often 80% on whichever project is loudest that week.

You might be wondering how to fix this without reorganizing your entire studio. The answer isn't a perfect org chart. It's explicit allocation agreements with clear escalation rules.

Write it down. "Alex is available to Game B for no more than 12 hours per week during Q3." When Game A needs Alex for more than that, it goes through you, not through Slack at 11pm.

Track allocation in real time. **Float** is purpose-built for this. **Teamwork** also handles resource scheduling well. If someone is consistently over-allocated across projects, that's not a people problem. It's a planning problem that you own.

---

## Run Separate Rhythms, Share One Cadence

Each game needs its own sprint cycle, its own standups, its own retrospectives. Don't collapse these. A Game A problem discussed in a Game B standup wastes everyone's time and blurs accountability.

But you also need one shared cadence at the producer level. I run a weekly portfolio sync, usually 30 minutes, just with leads. We look at the dashboard together. We surface cross-project issues. We make resource tradeoff decisions in that room, not in hallway conversations.

This rhythm keeps each team feeling like their project matters, while giving you the cross-project visibility to actually manage tradeoffs fairly.

---

## Decision Triage: How to Prioritize When Everything Feels Urgent

Here's what I tell people who are drowning in competing demands: not every urgent thing is important, and not every important thing feels urgent.

Use a simple triage framework for cross-title decisions:

| Priority | Criteria | Response Time |
|---|---|---|
| P1 | Blocks a milestone, submission, or launch | Same day |
| P2 | Impacts team velocity or morale within the next sprint | Within 48 hours |
| P3 | Important but can be scheduled | Next weekly sync |
| P4 | Nice to have, no current deadline pressure | Backlog review |

When you get a Slack message saying "we need to talk about the combat system," that's not automatically P1. Train your leads to label requests before they hit you. It saves enormous mental bandwidth.

## FAQ

### How many games can one producer realistically manage at once?

Most experienced producers max out at three titles in active development before quality of oversight starts dropping. Two is sustainable. Three requires excellent delegation and very healthy teams. Four is usually a studio org problem masquerading as a producer capacity problem.

### Should I use the same project management tool across all titles?

Yes, with customized templates per title. Consistency in tooling reduces context-switching cost. Using **Jira** for one game and **Trello** for another forces you to mentally shift systems every time you switch contexts. Pick one platform and customize it.

### How do I handle a crisis on one title without abandoning the others?

Appoint a deputy. Every title should have someone who can run the day-to-day for 48 to 72 hours without you. If that person doesn't exist, building them is your next priority, before the crisis happens.

### My teams are competing for my attention. How do I stay fair?

Block calendar time for each project every week, and protect it. If Game A always gets the Monday morning slot and Game B gets Wednesday afternoon, your teams see consistency. It also forces you to batch decisions rather than context-switching constantly.

### What's the most common thing that tanks multi-title productions?

Unclear ownership. When two projects share a producer and something goes wrong, both teams assume the other project got the attention. Document your ownership model explicitly. Who owns what decision, on which project, and what requires your escalation. Ambiguity at the producer level spreads into every team below it.

---

Multi-title production doesn't have to be controlled chaos. It's a learnable discipline, built on systems, clear communication, and the willingness to say "that's not a P1" out loud, even when people wish it were. Start with the portfolio view, protect your shared resources, and build the deputy relationships before you need them. The teams depending on you deserve a producer who can see the whole board, not just the loudest square on it.
