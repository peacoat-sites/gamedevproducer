You submitted your demo on June 14th, stayed up until the event went live, and spent the next seven days watching your Steam page like it owed you money. You refreshed the follower count every few hours. You told yourself 4,000 new followers would mean you were on track. By June 22nd, you had 340. And somewhere across town, another solo dev had the exact same experience and is now wondering if they picked the wrong week to quit their job.

That's not a failure story unique to you. That's June 2026's Steam Next Fest, and it just reset the math for everyone.

## The Numbers Are Real and They're Uncomfortable

| Metric | June 2025 | June 2026 | Change |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Total Demos | 2,645 | 4,382 | +66% |
| Top 10% Follower Gains | 163 | 121 | -25% |
| Top 1% Follower Gains | 1,759 | 1,330 | -24% |
| Godot Demo Share | 9.2% | 12.6% | +3.4pp |

This edition was the largest Steam Next Fest since Valve launched the event in 2020. According to [GameDiscoverCo analyst Simon Carless](https://newsletter.gamediscover.co/p/who-won-june-2026s-steam-next-fest), 4,382 playable demos ran across the June 15–22 window, up 66% from 2,645 demos in June 2025. That's not a gradual drift. That's a flood.

Here's what that flood did to outcomes. A game performing in the top 10% of Next Fest this June earned roughly 121 new Steam followers. One year ago, the same percentile position earned 163. That's a 25% drop in follower gains despite record total participation. Top 1% performers dropped from 1,759 new followers to 1,330 over the same period. The upside compressed even for the breakout titles.

What this tells you is that attention is now a fixed resource being split more ways every cycle. The wishlist and follower totals that signaled real traction a year ago now just mean you kept pace with a much bigger crowd.

## What "Good" Actually Looks Like Right Now

Chris Zukowski over at HowToMarketAGame.com has been tracking the wishlist benchmarks that indie studios need to launch viably, and his framing is useful here: 15,000 wishlists from a Next Fest used to be an exceptional result. Right now, it's the entry ticket. Not a sign you've made it. The floor for a launch worth taking seriously.

That number should sit with you for a minute, because I've seen a lot of developers treat 15,000 wishlists as a ceiling to aim for rather than a floor to clear. The difference between those two mental models is the difference between a studio that survives its first commercial release and one that learns an expensive lesson about launch week revenue.

What most people don't realize is that the raw follower numbers from Next Fest have always been a lagging indicator anyway. The studios that win a Next Fest don't usually win because their demo was available for seven days in June. They win because they spent the two months before the event building a content loop, a community, and a reason for players to care beyond the demo itself. The event is a multiplier. If you don't have anything to multiply, you get a small number fast.

## The One-Shot Problem Nobody Talks About Enough

Every game gets exactly one Next Fest per release cycle. February, June, or October: pick one, because that's your shot. The Steamworks documentation is clear on this, and Valve enforces it without exceptions.

That constraint makes this a genuinely high-stakes timing decision, and I'd argue most indie developers make it too casually. I've seen teams default to "the next available one" because they're excited and the demo is technically ready. What they don't account for is whether their marketing infrastructure is ready, whether they have streamer relationships warmed up, whether their store page copy is actually converting, or whether there's a content pipeline that can sustain momentum after the event closes.

The June 2026 edition landing at 4,382 demos should make you think harder about February and October. February historically runs smaller. October tends to get crowded with games chasing holiday season visibility. Neither is automatically better, but your game isn't average, and the right answer depends on your genre, your community size at the time, and frankly your own production timeline.

If your demo isn't ready to be good, wait. A demo that underperforms in Next Fest doesn't just waste the event slot. It trains Steam's algorithm that your game generates low engagement, and that's harder to recover from than most developers expect.

## The Engine Shift Is a Signal Worth Reading

This is a small detail that I think carries more weight than it's gotten credit for. Godot's share of Next Fest demos grew from 9.2% to 12.6% edition-over-edition, while Unity and Unreal both dipped slightly. That's not a blip. That's a visible structural shift in how indie developers are building games, likely accelerated by the Unity runtime fee controversy from 2023 and the licensing uncertainty that's continued to shape tooling decisions since.

Practically, this matters for a few reasons. A larger Godot developer base means a growing ecosystem of tutorials, plugins, and shared knowledge, which lowers the barrier to entry further. Lower barriers to entry mean more games. More games mean more Next Fest competition. The same forces making Godot a better choice for individual developers are contributing to the crowding that's compressing everyone's follower gains.

It's not a reason to avoid Godot. It's a reason to understand that the rising quality floor is industry-wide, and your demo needs to clear it regardless of what you built it in.

## Whether to Enter At All

The honest answer is that Next Fest is still worth it for most games, but not unconditionally. The bar for a demo that helps you has gotten higher. It needs to represent the actual feel of your game accurately, perform well technically, have a clear hook in the first two minutes, and exist within a broader marketing push that doesn't begin and end with the event itself.

If you have all of that, the event still delivers real visibility even with compressed per-game follower gains. The top performers in June 2026 still picked up 1,330 followers in a week, which is real momentum. The [Tech Insider coverage](https://tech-insider.org/steam-next-fest-june-2026/) noted the record scale of the event, which means the total player base browsing is also larger than it's ever been. More competition, but also more eyeballs.

If you don't have a demo that's ready to convert players into followers, and a marketing plan that surrounds the event, skipping the cycle and building toward a better-prepared entry is a legitimate strategy now in a way it wasn't two years ago. The math got harder. Your preparation has to match it.
