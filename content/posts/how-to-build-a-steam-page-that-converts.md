You spent eight months building a demo. You finally get it live on Steam, share the link everywhere, and... 127 wishlists in three weeks. Your friend's game, which honestly looks rougher than yours, already has 4,000. You refresh the page, wondering what you're missing. I've seen this exact situation more times than I can count, and almost every time the problem isn't the game. It's the page.

A Steam page is not a formality you fill out before the real marketing begins. It *is* the marketing, at least for the first 90% of your audience who will never see your tweets or your Discord. Valve's algorithm surfaces games to people who might like them, but it can only do that if your page clearly communicates what kind of game you made, and it can only convert those visitors into wishlists if every element earns the reader's trust fast. Most developers treat their Steam page like a README file. The ones who treat it like a landing page with a conversion goal are the ones who hit 10,000 wishlists before launch.

## The Capsule Image Is the Only Thing That Matters First

Everything else on your page is downstream of whether someone clicks through in the first place. Your capsule image (the 460x215 header graphic that shows up in search results and recommendation rows) is doing the entire job of a movie poster in a thumbnail the size of a postage stamp. If it doesn't communicate genre, tone, and visual quality within about half a second, people scroll past and never see your trailer, your description, or any of the work you put into the rest of the page.

What works in capsules: strong silhouette, a single dominant character or object, readable typography if you use any at all, and a color palette that creates contrast without looking like a ransom note. What doesn't work: group shots with five characters at equal size, screenshots used as capsules (this almost always looks amateurish), and logo-only designs with no visual context for what the game actually feels like.

Hire a graphic designer who has specifically done Steam capsules if you can. It's a niche skill. A general illustrator who is great at character art may produce a beautiful image that reads terribly at thumbnail scale. Budget somewhere between $150 and $500 for this if you're serious about it. Compare that to the cost of a single month of lost wishlist momentum and it's an easy decision.

## Your Trailer Has One Job: Make Someone Feel Something in 30 Seconds

The average person who lands on a Steam page and is not already sold on your game will watch somewhere between 30 and 60 seconds of your trailer before making a wishlist decision. Your trailer does not need to explain the lore. It does not need to show your character creation system or your skill tree. It needs to show the experience of playing your game, and it needs to do it immediately.

The opening 10 seconds are critical. Lead with the most visually compelling gameplay you have, not a studio logo, not a slow pan across scenery, not a title card. Actual, interesting gameplay. Show the moment that made your team excited when it first worked.

A few things I've seen trip up otherwise good trailers:

- Gameplay footage that's too clean. Players in alpha/beta playing your game are making interesting decisions. Staged "perfect run" footage often looks sterile and unconvincing.
- Audio that undersells the visuals. Sound design and music choice carry enormous emotional weight in trailers. Don't use free stock music because it was easy to license.
- No moment of clarity. Somewhere in the first 45 seconds, the viewer should understand the core loop. Not the whole game. Just: what do I do, what does it feel like, why is that interesting.

You don't need a $5,000 trailer. You need a trailer edited by someone who has watched a lot of good trailers and understands pacing. That person might be you, or it might be someone you pay $300 to on Fiverr. Watch the trailers for games similar to yours that you think did it well, and reverse-engineer their structure.

## Writing a Description That Actually Converts

Most game descriptions on Steam are written by developers who are too close to their own game. They lead with backstory, list features like a spec sheet, and bury the emotional hook. Here's the structure that tends to actually work:

**Line 1 (Short Description, 160 characters max):** One sentence. What is the core fantasy your game delivers? Not "a roguelike dungeon crawler with procedural generation." Something like: "Every run is a negotiation between your build and your paranoia." This shows up under search results. It needs to do real persuasive work.

**Opening paragraph of the full description:** The emotional promise. Why does this game exist? What feeling are you selling? Two to four sentences max before you get into anything mechanical.

**Feature bullets:** Use these, but write them as benefits, not features. Not "over 200 enemies." Instead: "Enemies that adapt to your tactics, so no two runs ever play the same way." The player reading this is asking one question: "Will I have a good time?" Every bullet should answer that.

**Closing paragraph:** Social proof if you have it (positive press quotes, demo player counts, awards), or a direct call to wishlist if you don't.

Keep paragraphs short. People skim. Use bold text for the first few words of each paragraph if it helps scannability. Write at a reading level that a tired person at 11pm can absorb without effort.

## Screenshots Are a Scroll-Stopper Problem

Steam allows up to 20 screenshots. Most developers upload 8 to 10. Most of those screenshots are generic gameplay captures that could be from any game in the genre. What you want instead is a curated set of 6 to 8 images that function like a visual argument for why your game is worth a person's time.

Think of it this way. Each screenshot should be making one specific point:

| Screenshot # | What it should communicate |
|---|---|
| 1 | The visual identity and art direction at their best |
| 2 | The core gameplay loop in action |
| 3 | A moment of player power or reward |
| 4 | Environmental depth or world-building |
| 5 | UI/readability (yes, this matters) |
| 6 | A surprising or unique mechanic |
| 7-8 | Emotional peak or story moment (if applicable) |

Don't include screenshots with debug overlays, low-rez placeholder assets, or unfinished UI. Don't include settings screens. Don't include your main menu. Every image is a sales asset.

## Tags, Metadata, and the Algorithm You Need to Understand

Valve's discovery system is a recommendation engine, and it uses your tags to understand what your game is and who to show it to. If your tags are wrong, or if you're trying to game the system by adding popular tags that don't accurately describe your game, the algorithm will surface your game to the wrong audience. Those people will bounce without wishlisting, and Valve's system will interpret that as a signal that your game is not relevant or interesting. It becomes a self-reinforcing problem.

Tag your game accurately and specifically. If you make a cozy farming sim with light combat, don't just tag "RPG" because it has bigger search volume than "Cozy." The players who search "Cozy" are the ones who will wishlist and buy your game. The players who search "RPG" looking for Dark Souls-adjacent content will leave immediately.

Beyond tags: fill out every metadata field Valve gives you. Developer name, publisher name, release date (even an approximate one like "Q3 2025"), languages, system requirements, content descriptors. A page with empty fields looks unfinished and can suppress visibility in certain regions or storefronts.

## When to Launch Your Steam Page (And What to Do After)

The short answer: earlier than you think. Steam pages accumulate wishlists over time, and Valve's algorithm rewards pages that show sustained momentum, not just a spike at launch. If your game is 12 months from release, your page should be up now. If it's 6 months out, your page should have been up 6 months ago.

The Next Fest strategy matters here. Steam Next Fest (runs a few times per year) is one of the most effective wishlist-building events available to indie developers. To participate, you need a public Steam page and a demo. If you're targeting a Next Fest, work backward: when is the fest, when does your page need to go live to build baseline visibility before the event, and when does your demo need to be ready. Developers who treat Next Fest casually and show up with a rough demo and 200 existing wishlists get a moderate bump. Developers who arrive with a polished demo and 2,000+ wishlists before the fest often leave with 10,000 to 30,000 more.

After your page goes live: link to it everywhere. Email list, social media, game dev forums, Reddit communities for your genre. Track your wishlist velocity in SteamDB (it's free and public). Understand your conversion rate: of all the people who visit your page, what percentage wishlist? If it's below 10%, something on the page is failing. Below 5% and you have a real problem that needs diagnosing before you spend another dollar on marketing.

Your Steam page is never truly done until your game is off sale forever. Treat it as a living document, review it quarterly if you're in a long development cycle, and get feedback on it from people who have never heard of your game before. The hardest part of this whole process is that you've been looking at your game so long that you've lost the ability to see it the way a stranger does. Find that stranger. Buy them a coffee. Ask them to narrate their thoughts as they scroll your page for the first time. That 10 minutes of feedback will be worth more than most of what you'll read about Steam marketing, including this article.
